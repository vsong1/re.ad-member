din·ner noun
from Old French disner, meaning "to break one's fast," from Latin disiejunare. It originally meant breakfast, or the first main meal eaten around midday, but changed to its current meaning over time as eating habits shifted.

On Tuesday evening, I decided to make dinner because the headache I'd had all afternoon finally faded enough to make the idea of chopping vegetables seem manageable. I still wasn't sure what caused my headache, although WebMD suggested it might be the excessive amount of caffeine that I consume, which seems plausible. I would blame that on Joyce though; if I did not have to cover for her every day, I wouldn't need to drink so much coffee.

I stood in front of the refrigerator with the door open longer than necessary, letting the cold air drift out over my feet. Inside, things looked as they usually do: a half-used bag of baby carrots slouched sideways on the second shelf, a carton of eggs from two weeks ago, and a package of chicken thighs that, according to the printed label, needed to be cooked by the following day. There was also a bunch of green onions lying diagonally across the top shelf, and an assortment of bottled and jarred condiments in the door, some nearly empty (soy sauce, ketchup, pesto) and other barely touched (ranch, ketchup, Tabasco, some fancy chili paste that I was gifted last Christmas). I tapped the chicken package lightly with my finger: that would be enough of a direction for the night.

The pan I use for most dinners was already out on the stove because I had washed it the night before and left it on the still-warm burner to dry. I adjusted its position and turned the dial just enough to make the ignition click softly—one, two, three times—before the flame appeared in that small blue half-ring that always looks slightly weaker than I expect it to.

The chicken thighs felt heavier than I remembered when I lifted them from the package. I trimmed a small strip of fat with scissors because the idea of taking out the cutting board felt like too much of an escalation for a Tuesday. The scissors stuck slightly with each snip, and I briefly wondered how many times I had trimmed things with them without washing them properly, but not enough to stop me. I salted the chicken with the same shaking motion I always use, a motion that never distributes the salt evenly and never bothers me enough to correct it.

When the oil in the pan began to shimmer subtly, I laid the chicken pieces in one by one. They made the familiar sizzling sound, though tentatively, like the chicken was not fully committed to being cooked yet. I pressed the pieces downward with the back of a fork to encourage contact with the pan, an action that always makes me feel strangely authoritative even though it achieves very little. A faint smell of raw poultry and heating oil rose up, the predictable prelude to something edible.

While the chicken browned, I turned back to the refrigerator to find something to accompany it. The baby carrots seemed like the easiest choice. They were slightly dried at the ends, with that pale, chalky look they get after sitting too long in the cold. I poured a handful into my palm—too many! I put two back-and set them beside the stove. I debated slicing them but concluded their current shape was acceptable. Dinner, on nights like this, is more about assembling heat and ingredients than creating any coherent dish.

I flipped the chicken over. The cooked side looked patchy, with a golden section near the center and a paler one near the edge. I pretended not to care about the unevenness, though I did feel a brief impulse to press down again with the fork. Instead, I added the carrots by simply scattering them around the chicken in a loose semicircle, like arranging props for a low-budget photograph. They rolled into place and made a small clattering sound against the pan, then settled into a steady, muted hiss.

The green onions came next. I grabbed them without rinsing—an act of mild culinary rebellion—and sliced them with the scissors directly over the pan. The pieces fell in floppy green rings, some landing neatly, others bouncing off the chicken and falling onto the stove. One piece landed near the burner grate, and I watched it curl slightly as if startled. I brushed it back into the pan with the handle of the fork and felt disproportionately accomplished.

As everything cooked, a thin fog of steam rose and condensed on the microwave door above the stove. I always notice this because the moisture dries unevenly, leaving streaks that I never quite manage to clean. The sound of the pan shifted from a sharp sizzle to a more diffuse bubbling, which told me—without needing to look—that the chicken had begun releasing its own moisture. The carrots, meanwhile, were softening in an uneven, haphazard way, some touching the hottest parts of the pan, others drifting toward cooler spots as if trying to avoid responsibility.

When the chicken looked finished—firm, lightly browned, giving off that faint, familiar sheen—I turned off the burner and let everything sit in the pan for a minute. I don’t know when I adopted the habit of letting food rest in the pan, but doing so always feels faintly professional, even when the meal itself looks like something improvised by someone halfway through moving apartments.

I spooned the chicken and vegetables into a bowl. The bowl was slightly too small, so one carrot rolled out and onto the counter. I placed it back on top with the sense of restoring order, even though it made no difference to the meal. I ate at the table without adding sauce or seasoning beyond the initial salt—partly out of laziness, partly because none of the sauces in the fridge seemed worth opening for a meal this unambitious.

The food tasted exactly like what it was: chicken, carrots, green onion, pan heat. Nothing surprising, nothing disappointing. It filled the space of the evening just enough to feel like dinner had occurred, without imposing any larger meaning. When I was finished, I rinsed the bowl, washed the fork, and wiped the counter. The pan stayed on the stove to dry, exactly as it had the night before.

And that was dinner.
